- In contrast, in the high-stress regimes or outliers, the linear model's mean absolute error swells to 40-90 EUR/MWh, proving that structural transitions degrade linear forecasts and that non-linear regimes exist and are successfully detected by UMAP/HDBSCAN density cuts.

This section provides the rigorous academic and theoretical justification for the unsupervised, density-based regime discovery approach, formatted for direct integration into a research paper or thesis literature review.

### 1. Theoretical Grounding: The Regime-Switching Paradigm in Energy Economics
Electricity spot prices do not follow a single continuous process. Due to the **non-storable nature of electricity** and **highly inelastic short-term demand**, prices are formed along a highly convex supply curve (the merit-order curve). Shifts in underlying fundamentals (fuel prices, renewable generation, demand shocks) trigger abrupt transitions between distinct market states, known as **Regimes** (Karakatsani & Bunn, 2008). 

Traditional finance models utilize parametric Markov Regime-Switching (MRS) models (Huisman & Mahieu, 2003). However, these parametric models:
1. Suffer from high computational complexity when dealing with multiple continuous fundamentals.
2. Rely on strict, often incorrect assumptions about the probability distributions of each regime.
3. Are prone to "data leakage" (target snooping) if regimes are identified directly from the price series itself before forecasting.

Our approach addresses these limitations by utilizing a **non-parametric, unsupervised density-based clustering framework (UMAP + HDBSCAN)** on the space of fundamental market drivers ($X_{fundamentals} = \{TTF\_Gas\_Price, Residual\_Load, EU\_Gas\_Storage\_Anomaly\}$). This identifies structural regimes purely from the physical topology of the market, ensuring zero target leakage and capturing non-linear interactions without assuming prior distributions.

```latex
To model the structural changes in electricity price formation, we reject the single-regime linearity assumption. Following Ertugrul et al. (2023) and Weron (2014), we frame the electricity market as a multi-regime system characterized by structural breaks. Instead of utilizing target-dependent parametric Markov Regime-Switching models (Huisman & Mahieu, 2003), which are prone to target leakage and require restrictive distribution assumptions, we propose an unsupervised, density-based topological regime discovery framework. 

First, let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$ represent the matrix of physical market fundamentals, where the features $D$ include the gas price ($TTF$), the residual load ($L_{res}$), and the gas storage deviation from historical norm ($S_{dev}$). To capture non-linear, high-dimensional density structures, UMAP is applied to project the scaled fundamental space $X$ into a low-dimensional manifold $Y \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times 2}$. 

HDBSCAN is subsequently fitted on $Y$ using data-driven constrained hyperparameters ($2 \le C \le 8$), segmenting the manifold into distinct high-density clusters representing structural market states (regimes):
\begin{equation}
R_i = \text{HDBSCAN}(Y_i)
\end{equation}
where $R_i \in \{-2, -1, 0, 1, ..., K\}$ represents the assigned regime, with $-2$ designating extreme anomalies detected via an isolation forest filter, $-1$ representing unclassified noise, and $\{0, ..., K\}$ representing base, spike, and drop regimes as defined by Janczura & Weron (2010). The resulting labels are fed as exogenous categorical variables into a downstream gradient-boosting framework (XGBoost), enabling the model to dynamically shift its non-linear predictive pathways based on the discovered physical topology of the market.
```
